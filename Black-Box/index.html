Abstract


The susceptibility of DNNs to adversarial attacks in Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) systems has gained many concerns in recent years. Current work on generating adversarial attacks for ASR systems has focused on white-box methods under a fallacious assumption that the architecture and parameters of a DNN model are known. While limited black-box attack methods have been proposed in recent years, they mainly rely on a query-based manner. Typically, the query-based black-box attack mixes a genetic algorithm (GA) with gradient estimation (GE), in which the GA provides a global search to explore the target space and the GE provides a local search when an adversarial sample nears its target perturbation. However, experiments show that the existing query-based methods fail to find any adversarial example for long audio samples in an ASR system. The traditional GA is frequently stuck in some local regions without any adversarial example in a huge search space. This paper proposes a new black-box approach, called DE-gradient, by introducing the differential evolution (DE) that is a population-based search algorithm. We compared DE-gradient with two black-box attack methods on two public audio datasets. Experimental results show that DE-gradient improves the success rate of attacks by 19% and increases the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of silent sections from 27 dB to 54 dB. DE-gradient can provide a good baseline for evaluating the black-box adversarial attack of ASR systems.


Introduction

Welcome! On this page, we demonstrate our DE-gradient algorithm alongside two other attack methods (GA-gradient and PSO) as applied to eight audio samples from the Mozilla Common Voice dataset. We provide explanations of the metrics and corresponding audio samples for listening in the subsequent content.


Metrics


Speech Similarity. We use the Pearson correlation to measure the similarity between adversarial examples and the original samples. Given the speech samples \( x \) and its adversarial samples \( x_{adv} \), the formula of correlation coefficient \( \rho \) is: \[ \rho_{x,x_{adv}} = \frac{cov(x,x_{adv})}{\delta_x\delta_{adv}}, \] where \( cov(\cdot) \) represents the covariance between the original speech samples and the adversarial examples, and \( \delta_x \) (\( \delta_{adv} \)) represents the standard deviation of the samples (the adversarial examples \( x_{adv} \)). A higher \( \rho \) indicates that the generated adversarial examples are more similar to the original audio samples.

Running Time. The running time \( t \) of each algorithm is given by \( t_e-t_b \), where \( t_b \) and \( t_e \) are the start time and the end time, respectively.

Signal-to-Noise Ratio. We use the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) to analyze the difference between the original audio and the adversarial example in the silent part. The formula of SNR is \[ SNR = 10\log_{10}(\frac{P_{signal}}{P_{noise}}), \] where \( P_{signal} \) is the power of the original audio, and \( P_{noise} \) is the power of the adversarial noise over each adversarial example. A large \( SNR \) value indicates a small noise scale. We selected 0.1 seconds from the head of each audio adversarial example with two datasets as the silent part.
